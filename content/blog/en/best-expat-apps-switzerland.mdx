## Why an expat app Switzerland setup matters in the first 90 days

The first three months after arriving in Switzerland are usually the most admin-heavy. You may need to complete your **Anmeldung** at the local Gemeinde or Einwohnerkontrolle, arrange compulsory health insurance under KVG/LAMal within three months, open a bank account, set up mobile service, understand waste disposal rules, and, depending on your permit status, deal with migration office requests or employer paperwork.

The problem is not just that there are many tasks. The problem is that each task depends on another one. You may need your rental contract for registration. You may need your registration confirmation to complete other processes. You may need insurance proof, ID, work contract, and local address details in different combinations, depending on canton and provider.

That is why a strong app setup is so useful. Instead of relying on memory, scattered notes, and browser tabs, good expat apps help you do three things:

## Best apps for transport, navigation, and daily logistics

If you live in Switzerland, transport apps are essential from day one. The most important one is the official **SBB Mobile** app. Even if you do not travel by train every day, SBB Mobile is incredibly useful because it covers much more than long-distance trains. It includes local trains, buses, trams, platforms, route planning, disruptions, and ticket purchases. For many newcomers, it becomes the most-used app in the first month.

For navigation, **Google Maps** is still practical, especially for business listings, opening hours, and walking directions. In some cases, it is worth cross-checking addresses against official listings, especially for government offices, but for day-to-day navigation it remains useful.

## Best apps for banking, insurance, and settling in

Once the first registration tasks are done, the next priorities usually involve money and health. Switzerland has a high cost of living, and the health insurance system is mandatory. Newcomers quickly discover that choosing the wrong provider, misunderstanding deductible options, or delaying enrollment can create unnecessary costs.

A finance stack for expats in Switzerland usually includes:

- a banking app from your chosen bank or financial service,
- budgeting or expense tracking if you are still learning local prices,
- an insurance comparison workflow or organized research notes,
- a relocation guide that explains how the system actually works.

Health insurance deserves special attention. Under KVG/LAMal, basic health insurance is compulsory for residents in Switzerland. You usually need to register within three months of arrival. Premiums vary by canton, insurer, age, and model, while the benefits of the statutory basic package are legally standardized. What changes is often the premium, deductible, and access model such as standard, HMO, family doctor, or telemedicine.

Most insurer apps are only useful after you have chosen a provider. Before that stage, what people need is understanding. That is another reason an app like Sweezy is helpful: it can guide you through the logic of the system, explain what to compare, and keep track of tasks linked to insurance, doctor registration, and supporting documents.

For work and career, the built-in CV builder is also practical. Switzerland often expects a more locally adapted application style than some newcomers are used to. If you are job hunting after relocation, having a dedicated Swiss-style CV workflow inside the same ecosystem is a real advantage.
